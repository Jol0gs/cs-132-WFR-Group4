Background

Poverty is one of the oldest and most pressing societal problems, and it is clearly evident in the Philippine context. Reducing poverty is a main goal of the United Nations' SDG 1 (No Poverty), which pushes for equal opportunities and a better quality of life for everyone. Despite years of development, poverty in the country is still closely tied to income inequality, inflation, regional differences, and employment challenges that affect many Filipino families.

To better understand and address these issues, national and international institutions like the Philippine Statistics Authority (PSA), Asian Development Bank (ADB), and World Bank regularly track and publish data related to poverty. By combining and analyzing these datasets, we can gain clearer insights into the factors and trends behind poverty in the country and come up with more effective, data-driven solutions.

Research Questions

1. Trend Analysis: How does the poverty headcount ratio in the Philippines change over time?

2. Poverty Determinants: What demographic or economic factors (e.g., GDP per capita, employment rate) are correlated with changes in poverty levels?

3. Poverty Forecasting: How can recent trends of these factors be used to predict the poverty headcount for 2025?

Hypotheses

Null Hypothesis (H0): No significant correlation exists between the selected economic factors and poverty levels.

Alternative Hypothesis (H1): There is a significant correlation between the selected economic factors and poverty levels.

Data Collection & Repository

The team gathered data from both national and international sources that provide data on income, poverty incidence, country-level aggregates, and other economic indicators to ensure the reliability and comprehensiveness of our analysis. Primary sources included the Philippine Statistics Authority (PSA), through its Poverty Statistics and Family Income and Expenditure Survey (FIES), complemented by datasets from the Asia Data Portal, Asian Development Bank (ADB), and Mendeley Data. These datasets were cleaned and combined to ensure consistency across indicators, helping us analyze poverty trends and the socioeconomic factors influencing them in the Philippines.

Hypothesis Testing

We used spearman correlation tests for the hypothesis testing for this study. HOwever, we reiterate that we were limited to 24 samples after cleaning the data, so we proceed with caution in obtaining interpretations for the data.

As shown, there is significant negative correlation between GDP and poverty(p-value< 0.0004, which was actually truncated by python for some reason), implying that with a higher GDP, we can expect poverty to be lower. Surprisingly, Employment does not have a significant enough correlation to say that it significantly affects poverty(p-value=0.693). This may be due to other factors such as the disparity between the rich and the poor or the wages of the employed aren't that high compared to a minimum standard.

Conclusion

In concluding our results, we would also like to express our overall findings, which includes the datasets we gathered and possible interpretation of the results obtained from processing the data.

First of all, From the datasets we gathered, we can observe that poverty in the Philippines has been decreasing over the years, especially after 2014. This is may be a result of various economic factors, however, take this with a pinch of salt since as stated earlier, the number of samples we obtained after cleaning amounts to 24

From the hypothesis testing we conducted, we can conclude that GDP has a significant negative correlation with poverty, which may imply that as the country's economy grows, poverty decreases. On the other hand, employment does not appear to have a significant correlation with poverty, which may be due to other factors such as wage disparity or underemployment. These factors can additionally improve the reliability of results and further explain the gaps in this study

Overall, while our findings provide some insights into the relationship between economic factors and poverty in the Philippines, it is important to consider the limitations of our data and analysis. Further research with more comprehensive datasets and advanced analytical techniques may be needed to fully understand the complexities of poverty in the country. And moreover, we observed that international data is more reliable, consistent, and easier to interpret compared to national data, which may have discrepancies or missing values that can affect the analysis.

With this, we encourage future researchers to take our warning into account that available national data has multiple caveats, and try to find readily available data that is consistent as national datasets aren't that reliable(Contrary to expectations)
